# R&D Nordic AI Services Menu

Build your own programme from a 3 to 10 module menu. Each module can stand alone, but they work best when combined into a focused pathway for capability building, operational improvement, and governance support.

## Foundations

### 1. AI Workbench Setup
Promise: Help your team work inside an IDE with less friction, fewer naming mistakes, and a cleaner project structure from day one.

What it covers: This module sets up the practical basics for AI-supported work in an IDE, including project trees, file naming, core operational documents such as `agents.md`, `claude.md`, `session-handoff.md`, `lessons-learned.md`, and `orchestrator.md`, and the routines that stop projects becoming messy after the first burst of enthusiasm.

Who it is for: Teams starting to use Claude, Codex, or similar tools in live delivery work and needing a stable operating baseline.

### 2. Prompting for Repeatable Results
Promise: Improve output quality by turning one-off prompting into a repeatable working method.

What it covers: This module focuses on how to write instructions that produce more reliable, on-brand, and task-appropriate outputs across drafting, analysis, implementation support, and review. It covers prompt structure, role framing, constraints, examples, and how to reduce drift between users.

Who it is for: Professionals who already use AI tools but want more consistency and less trial-and-error.

### 3. From Task to Workflow
Promise: Show your team what an automated workflow really looks like before they overcomplicate it.

What it covers: This module explains the core mechanics behind useful AI workflows: task decomposition, sub-agents, handoffs, orchestration, checkpoints, and where simple pipelines beat ambitious but fragile automation. It gives teams a grounded view of what should be automated and what should stay human-led.

Who it is for: Teams moving from experimentation to practical process design.

### 4. Team Style Guide for AI Output
Promise: Keep AI-assisted writing and delivery aligned with your brand, standards, and internal expectations.

What it covers: This module helps define how AI-generated outputs should sound, look, and behave across documents, decks, summaries, and client-facing communication. It covers tone, formatting rules, approval expectations, escalation language, and style guardrails for both internal and external use.

Who it is for: Organisations that need AI outputs to feel coherent across teams, departments, or external-facing materials.

## Build and Scale

### 5. Claude and Codex in Practice
Promise: Help your team choose the right tool, mode, and workflow for the job instead of using everything for everything.

What it covers: This module gives a practical overview of how tools such as Claude and Codex differ in use, where skills and plugin-style extensions fit, when to use build-oriented workflows, and how to decide between direct prompting, structured tasking, and agent-based execution.

Who it is for: Teams comparing tools or trying to standardise how they use them in delivery work.

### 6. Sub-Agent and Handoff Design
Promise: Build clearer internal delegation patterns so AI-supported work scales without confusion.

What it covers: This module covers how to split work into meaningful components, define responsibilities, manage handoffs, avoid duplicated effort, and document the state of play between sessions. It is about disciplined coordination, not novelty for its own sake.

Who it is for: Teams running multi-step work across projects, contributors, or repeated client assignments.

### 7. Automation Pipeline Blueprint
Promise: Turn promising AI use cases into a workable delivery pipeline with fewer dead ends.

What it covers: This module maps a target workflow from intake to output, identifies what should be templated, structured, reviewed, and approved, and defines a realistic first pipeline that can be tested without excessive technical overhead. Typical examples include briefing-to-deck, intake-to-summary, and structured review flows.

Who it is for: Organisations that want a practical first automation pipeline rather than a vague innovation roadmap.

### 8. Knowledge Files and Operational Memory
Promise: Reduce repeat mistakes and improve continuity across sessions, people, and projects.

What it covers: This module introduces lightweight operational memory practices, including lessons learned, session handoff notes, reusable instructions, reference files, and documentation patterns that make AI-assisted work more stable over time.

Who it is for: Teams that lose context between sessions or want a stronger continuity model for ongoing AI-supported work.

## Governance and Leadership

### 9. GDPR and Data Handling for AI Workflows
Promise: Help your organisation use AI in ways that support stronger privacy practice and better data discipline.

What it covers: This module covers the privacy basics that matter when introducing AI into operational workflows, including data minimisation, lawful basis, processor awareness, data-sharing boundaries, retention thinking, and where documentation should exist before scaling a process.

Who it is for: Public sector teams, regulated organisations, and businesses handling personal or sensitive information.

### 10. EU AI Act and Responsible Use Readiness
Promise: Build internal readiness for AI governance without turning the process into theatre.

What it covers: This module provides a practical introduction to AI governance expectations, including risk thinking, documentation habits, role clarity, oversight, transparency considerations, and the difference between policy language and operational reality. It supports preparation, not legal sign-off.

Who it is for: Leaders and delivery teams that need a grounded approach to responsible AI adoption.

### 11. DPIA, DPA, ROPA and Documentation Support
Promise: Strengthen the supporting documentation around AI-related projects so decisions are easier to defend and review.

What it covers: This module helps teams understand when structured documentation may be needed and how records such as DPIAs, DPAs, ROPAs, ethical self-review notes, and related governance materials can support better decisions, cleaner approvals, and stronger internal accountability.

Who it is for: Teams building new AI-supported processes that need clearer governance artefacts around them.

### 12. Executive Alignment and Adoption Strategy
Promise: Give decision-makers a clearer basis for choosing where AI should help, where it should not, and how to lead adoption credibly.

What it covers: This module focuses on leadership choices: priority setting, use-case selection, adoption risks, capability expectations, assurance questions, and how to align operational teams with management without creating a gap between ambition and delivery.

Who it is for: Senior leaders, programme owners, and managers responsible for AI direction and organisational uptake.
